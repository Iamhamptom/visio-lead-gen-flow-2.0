## 1. Market Structure — the franchise oligopoly and the long tail

South Africa's residential real estate agency market is structured around a small number of franchise networks, a handful of large independents, and a long tail of indie agents and one-person operations. Every practising agent must be registered with the **Property Practitioners Regulatory Authority (PPRA)**, the successor to the Estate Agency Affairs Board, under the **Property Practitioners Act 22 of 2019** (effective February 2022).

- **PPRA registration is mandatory.** Every estate agent must hold a valid Fidelity Fund Certificate (FFC) issued annually by the PPRA, must complete the prescribed Continuing Professional Development (CPD), and must comply with the FICA / anti-money-laundering reporting obligations imposed on the property sector.
- **Franchise networks dominate brand visibility**: Pam Golding Properties, Seeff Property Group, RE/MAX SA, Chas Everitt International, Harcourts SA, Rawson Properties, Engel &amp; Völkers SA, ERA SA, Just Property, and Sotheby&apos;s International Realty SA collectively account for the majority of national listings on Property24 and Private Property.
- **Pam Golding Properties** is widely regarded as the largest premium-tier residential agency in SA, with the deepest network in the high-net-worth Sandton/Bryanston/Constantia markets.
- **Seeff Property Group** is the largest by branch count nationally, with strong coverage across coastal and inland metros.
- **RE/MAX SA** is the largest by registered-agent count, with a franchise model that produces a wide geographic footprint.

### Listing portals — the discovery layer the entire market depends on

- **Property24** &mdash; the dominant listing portal, owned by Naspers (Prosus). Carries the overwhelming majority of national listings and is the de facto demand-side platform for SA buyers.
- **Private Property** &mdash; the second-largest listing portal, with a stronger HNW-tier presence.
- **MyProperty** and other niche portals form a long tail.

Both Property24 and Private Property are themselves substantial businesses; the fees they charge agents to feature listings are a meaningful operating cost line for the franchise networks.

## 2. The agent commission model — how the money flows

The economics of an SA estate agency are well understood and publicly disclosed in agent recruitment marketing.

- **Standard commission rate**: typically **5%–7.5% of the sale price** (excluding VAT) on residential transactions, paid by the seller out of the proceeds at registration of transfer at the Deeds Office.
- **VAT** at 15% is added to the commission and paid to SARS by the agency.
- **Commission split between franchise / agent**: varies by network and seniority. Common splits are **50/50 to 70/30** in favour of the agent, with senior agents in premium brands earning at the top of the range.
- **On a R2m sale at 6% commission**, the gross commission is **R120,000** plus VAT. After a 60/40 agent split, the listing agent earns **R72,000** in gross income, less their personal cost-of-doing-business.
- **Time-to-cash** for the agent: typically **8–12 weeks** between offer-to-purchase signature and Deeds Office registration, depending on bond approval, conveyancing, and rates clearance. Until registration, the agent has done the work but has not been paid.

**Implications for lead pricing**: a warm seller lead that converts to a sole mandate on a R2m property generates **R60k–R100k of agent gross income** at registration. At a 20% lead-to-mandate conversion rate, the break-even lead price is in the R12k–R20k band. Lead-market prices for residential real estate in SA are observed in the **R250–R1,200** band, with premium leads (HNW listings, sole mandates, sellers in fast-moving suburbs) fetching the top of the range.

## 3. The SARB rate cycle and the residential market response

Residential real estate is the most rate-sensitive consumer market in SA, and the 2024–2026 cycle has been the most decisive cycle since the post-COVID rebound.

- **SARB cut the repo rate by a cumulative 150 basis points** between September 2024 and early 2026, bringing prime from 11.75% to 10.25%.
- On a **R1.5M bond at 20 years**, this 150bps cut reduces the monthly instalment by approximately **R1,500/month** &mdash; restoring R18,000 per annum of household disposable income for the median first-time homebuyer.
- The rate cut translates almost directly into qualifying-buyer affordability: every 50bps of cut raises the qualifying buyer pool meaningfully, particularly at the entry-level R800k–R1.5M band where first-time buyers cluster.
- **ooba Oobarometer Q4 2025** reports that the first-time buyer share of applications hit **~48% &mdash; the highest in over a decade**, with median first-time buyer purchase prices around R1.2M.
- **BetterBond Property Market Report Q4 2025** flagged rising sub-R1.5M application volume in Gauteng East Rand and Vaal Triangle corridors, confirming where the volume is concentrating.

**VRL thesis**: the rate cycle has produced the strongest residential market in SA since 2021, but the volume is concentrated in the R800k–R2.5M price band where first-time buyers and second-step upgraders dominate. Premium-tier (R5m+) volume has lagged the recovery and remains a slower segment.

## 4. Two-Pot Retirement System as a residential deposit engine

Independent of the rate cycle, the **Two-Pot Retirement System** (effective 1 September 2024) has quietly become a major source of first-time-buyer deposit funding.

- **R57 billion withdrawn** from two-pot savings by **June 2025** (Moonstone Information Refinery, citing Treasury and ASISA data).
- **Over 2.4 million valid withdrawal tax directives** approved by SARS by end-January 2025.
- A typical two-pot withdrawal of **R80,000–R150,000** is enough to fund a **10% deposit on an R800k–R1.5M property** plus transfer and bond registration costs &mdash; precisely the price band where first-time buyers cluster.
- **79% of debt-settlement two-pot withdrawers plan repeat withdrawals**, indicating an ongoing structural cash-injection cycle into household balance sheets.
- The funds typically arrive as a lump-sum credit into the household&apos;s cheque account rather than as a disclosed retirement withdrawal, making the deposit invisible to bank origination scorecards but very visible as transaction-level evidence to a property-sector lead engine that can detect the underlying intent.

**VRL thesis**: two-pot is **silently underwriting a structural expansion of the first-time buyer pool** &mdash; visible in the ooba 48% FTB share but not yet quantified by formal industry research. Estate agents who can identify "two-pot ready" first-time buyers 30–90 days before they make an offer have a structural funnel advantage over agents who wait for the buyer to walk into a show-house cold.

## 5. Lightstone, Deeds Office and the data infrastructure

Unlike most SA consumer-finance markets, residential real estate has a deep public data infrastructure that makes signal generation unusually rich.

- **Deeds Office data** &mdash; every residential transfer is recorded at the Deeds Office and the data is publicly accessible via subscription services. Lightstone, PropStats and other data providers package this into agent-facing dashboards.
- **Lightstone Property** &mdash; the dominant residential property data provider in SA. Lightstone publishes property-level valuations, suburb-level price trends, and transaction histories used by agents, banks, and conveyancers.
- **Suburb-level performance data** &mdash; price growth, average days on market, sale-to-listing ratio &mdash; is granular enough to identify hot suburbs versus stagnant suburbs in real time.
- **The bond originator data layer** (ooba, BetterBond, MultiNET) provides upstream visibility into qualifying buyer behaviour at the application stage, before the buyer has even put in an offer.

**VRL observation**: a residential lead engine in SA has a richer underlying data infrastructure than almost any other consumer-services vertical because the Deeds Office is a public registry. This is the structural reason a high-quality residential lead aggregator is technically feasible in SA in a way it would not be in many adjacent markets.

## 6. Geographic concentration and Gauteng dominance

Residential real estate volume in SA is concentrated in three provincial hubs &mdash; Gauteng, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal &mdash; with Gauteng commanding the single largest share by both unit volume and total transaction value.

- **Gauteng share of national residential transfers**: approximately **36–40% by value** (informed estimate based on Lightstone provincial breakdowns; flag for verification against the latest Lightstone Annual Property Report).
- **Median Gauteng residential sale price 2025**: approximately **R1.35M** (versus R1.5M national, dragged up by Western Cape coastal stock).
- **Gauteng first-time buyer corridors**: Centurion, Midrand, Fourways, Sandton North, East Rand (Kempton Park, Edenvale, Boksburg, Alberton, Germiston), Roodepoort, Krugersdorp.
- **Gauteng premium corridors**: Sandton, Bryanston, Hyde Park, Houghton, Parkmore, Rosebank, Waterkloof, Brooklyn, Lynnwood Glen, Constantia Kloof.
- **Western Cape Atlantic Seaboard, Cape Winelands and Southern Suburbs** remain the highest-priced sub-markets in SA but with lower transaction volume than Gauteng.
- **KwaZulu-Natal North Coast** (Umhlanga, Ballito) is the fastest-growing premium sub-market, driven by Gauteng-to-KZN semigration.

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **SARB cumulative 150bps cut from September 2024 to early 2026** &mdash; the most aggressive loosening cycle in years and the dominant driver of the residential recovery.
- **ooba Q4 2025 first-time buyer share at ~48%** &mdash; the highest in over a decade, confirming the structural FTB surge.
- **Two-pot withdrawals hit R57bn by June 2025** &mdash; the silent first-time-buyer deposit engine.
- **Property Practitioners Act compliance cycle** &mdash; PPRA enforcement of FFC requirements and CPD has been visible through 2024–2025, with non-compliant agents being suspended from practising.
- **Pam Golding, Seeff, RE/MAX, Chas Everitt all reported strong 2025 listing growth** in trade press, confirming the macro recovery on the supply side.
- **Property24 listings volume** continued to grow through 2025, with Gauteng entry-level segments showing the fastest listing turnover.
- **Semigration to KZN North Coast and Cape Town** continued, with Gauteng losing population to coastal metros at the upper end while gaining first-time-buyer households at the entry level.
- **Investor buy-to-let activity** picked up in 2025 as the rate cycle improved residential rental yields in select suburbs, particularly student accommodation in Pretoria, Hatfield, and Auckland Park.
