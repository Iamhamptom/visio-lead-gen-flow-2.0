## 1. Market Structure — the Takealot-Amazon era

South Africa's e-commerce market has, within 18 months, gone from a single-dominant-platform market (Takealot) to a genuinely contested two-horse platform market (Takealot + Amazon SA), with a long tail of specialist retailers, grocery-delivery apps, and DTC brands around them.

- **Takealot Group (owned by Naspers / Prosus)** — the historical platform leader in SA, including Takealot.com, Superbalist (fashion), Mr D Food, and Takealot's third-party marketplace. Takealot is by far the most-visited e-commerce site in SA and its Black Friday and Blue Dot Sale events are the most-watched retail events in the SA calendar.
- **Amazon South Africa** — launched **May 2024**, marking the most significant structural change in SA e-commerce since Takealot's founding. Amazon opened with a local fulfilment network and Prime-style service commitments, creating direct competitive pressure on Takealot's third-party marketplace.
- **Grocery delivery apps** have emerged as a parallel e-commerce layer: **Checkers Sixty60** (Shoprite Holdings) dominates the SA on-demand grocery market; **Pick n Pay asap!** and **Woolworths online** compete in the premium tier; **OneCart** (Massmart / Makro) and **Bottles** (Heineken-owned alcohol delivery).
- **Fashion and specialty retailers** with direct e-commerce operations: **Superbalist**, **Bash** (TFG), **Mr Price online**, **Zando**, **Edgars online**, **Yuppiechef** (kitchenware specialist), **Makro** (general merchandise).
- **Long tail of DTC and Shopify stores** — a large and growing population of independent SA brands running on Shopify Plus, WooCommerce, or custom stacks. Categories include skincare, beverages, fitness apparel, supplements, pet care, home goods, and niche hobby products.
- **Marketplaces and classifieds** — **Bob Shop** (formerly bidorbuy), **Property24**, **Cars.co.za**, and **Gumtree** operate parallel commerce layers in specific verticals.

## 2. Market size, growth, and the "R71bn and rising" figure

SA e-commerce market sizing is one of the most commonly cited but least independently verified figures in SA retail commentary.

- The **Worldwide Worx Online Retail Report** (published annually by World Wide Worx, typically sponsored by Mastercard and Visa) is the single most-cited market-size source. The 2023 edition widely reported **SA online retail passing R71 billion** in 2023 — a figure that has become the standard industry shorthand, though it should be flagged as publisher-reported rather than independently audited.
- Year-on-year growth through 2022-2024 was repeatedly cited in the 20-30% range, well ahead of general retail growth.
- **E-commerce as a percentage of total SA retail** was widely reported at 5–6% by the end of 2023, rising through 2024-2025 but still materially lower than global benchmarks (UK, US, China sit in the 15–30% range).
- The **launch of Amazon SA in May 2024** is widely expected to accelerate the penetration curve because it brings international-tier logistics expectations (Prime delivery, category breadth, pricing discipline) to the SA market.
- **Post-launch commentary in 2025 trade press** suggested Amazon SA was gaining share faster in specific categories (books, tech, some household goods) while Takealot retained dominance in broader general merchandise.

**VRL caveat**: the R71bn+ headline figure should be cited only with its source attribution and year. Market-sizing figures for SA e-commerce vary widely between publishers (Worldwide Worx, Statista, GfK, Kantar) and should never be quoted as a single consensus figure.

## 3. Regulation — ECT Act, CPA, POPIA, NCC

E-commerce in South Africa operates under four overlapping consumer-protection regimes, none of which individually dominates but which collectively define what online sellers can and cannot do.

- **Electronic Communications and Transactions Act 25 of 2002 (ECT Act)** — the foundational e-commerce legislation, establishing the legal recognition of electronic contracts, digital signatures, consumer cooling-off rights (Section 44, 7-day cooling-off for unsolicited electronic transactions), and the legal framework for online consumer protection. The ECT Act is administered by the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies.
- **Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (CPA)** — governs all consumer transactions, online and offline. The CPA imposes minimum disclosure requirements, prohibits misleading advertising, and provides for the cooling-off right and the right to return faulty goods. The **National Consumer Commission (NCC)** administers the CPA and investigates complaints.
- **Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 (POPIA)** — governs the processing of personal information, including customer email addresses, delivery details, payment information, and behavioural tracking. POPIA compliance is a significant operational cost for SA e-commerce merchants.
- **National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA)** — relevant where e-commerce offers instalment credit (BNPL, layby, credit agreements at checkout). Payflex, Mobicred, Happy Pay and other BNPL providers sit inside this regime.

## 4. The economic anatomy of an SA e-commerce merchant

SA e-commerce merchants operate on margin structures that are broadly similar to their international counterparts but with local friction points that change the unit economics materially.

| Cost line | Typical % of revenue | SA-specific friction |
|---|---:|---|
| Cost of goods sold (COGS) | 45–60% | Import duties, weakening rand, supplier FX exposure |
| Fulfilment / shipping | 8–15% | Large, sparse country; Courier Guy / The Courier Guy / Aramex / PUDO dominance |
| Payment processing | 2.5–3.5% | Peach / Yoco / iKhokha / PayFast / Stitch fee bands |
| Platform commission (if marketplace) | 12–18% | Takealot / Amazon SA marketplace commission tiers |
| Advertising / acquisition | 10–25% | Meta + Google dominance, rising CPMs |
| Operations / staff | 8–15% | Customer service, warehousing, returns processing |
| Returns and refunds | 3–10% | CPA cooling-off rights, fashion returns are highest |

**The critical unit**: SA e-commerce **customer acquisition cost (CAC)** is the single most sensitive variable in the business. High-performing DTC merchants operate at CAC in the R150-R600 range (VRL observation from publicly discussed operator benchmarks). At those levels, lead-gen services that deliver warm intent at R100-R500 per lead can materially improve merchant unit economics &mdash; if the lead quality is high enough.

**VRL thesis**: the SA e-commerce merchant&apos;s biggest pain point is not traffic, it is **intent-scored traffic**. Any lead-gen layer that can distinguish a genuine buyer from a price-comparison browser is worth paying for on a pure CAC arithmetic basis.

## 5. Cart abandonment, intent signals, and the merchant-side signal gap

Cart abandonment is the single most-studied metric in global e-commerce. The typical abandonment rate sits in the 65-75% range globally; SA-specific figures are less well published but are widely cited as sitting in the same band or slightly higher due to payment-friction and delivery-confidence issues.

- A detected **cart abandonment** event is the highest-confidence buying intent signal in the e-commerce vertical &mdash; second only to an actual completed purchase.
- **Category research** (dwell time on product listing pages, repeat visits to specific SKUs, comparison across retailers) is a strong pre-abandonment signal.
- **Price comparison** behaviour (moving between Takealot, Amazon SA, Superbalist and a specialty retailer on the same product category) signals a buyer in active decision mode with a 7-30 day conversion window.
- **Review engagement** &mdash; reading product reviews, sorting reviews by rating, filtering reviews by specific keywords &mdash; is a late-stage trust-building signal that typically precedes purchase by 24-72 hours.
- **Delivery area checks** &mdash; the act of checking whether a delivery address is serviced &mdash; is a near-certain purchase signal with a 24-48 hour decay.
- **Repeat-purchase windows** &mdash; detected via the average replenishment cycle for specific consumable categories (beauty, supplements, pet food, household) &mdash; are highly predictable signals that merchants can use for proactive re-engagement.

**VRL thesis**: the SA e-commerce merchant ecosystem has rich first-party intent data but almost no infrastructure for sharing that data across merchants in a privacy-compliant way. A POPIA-compliant, consent-gated signal aggregation layer that shares <em>categories and intent stages</em> (not specific SKUs or PII) across a merchant panel creates category-level buyer-intent visibility that no individual merchant can reproduce alone.

## 6. The Amazon SA launch and its 2025 implications

The **May 2024 launch of Amazon SA** was the single most consequential SA e-commerce event of the decade and has reshaped merchant strategy conversations across the market.

- Amazon opened with a **local fulfilment network**, a **Prime-style service commitment**, and an **aggressive category onboarding push** &mdash; particularly in books, tech, home &amp; kitchen, and general merchandise.
- **Takealot&apos;s competitive response** through 2024-2025 included increased marketplace seller incentives, expanded Blue Dot Sale promotions, and deeper integration across the Naspers portfolio (Mr D, Superbalist, TakealotFresh).
- **Third-party sellers** now face a **choice architecture**: list exclusively on Takealot, exclusively on Amazon SA, or dual-list. Most serious SA sellers are moving to dual-listing, which creates a data-sharing challenge (neither platform wants to cede buyer-behaviour data to the other).
- **DTC brands** have been largely unaffected at the high end (premium DTC brands with direct customer relationships) but are feeling pricing pressure at the mid and low end where marketplace commoditisation is sharpest.
- **Small specialty retailers** have been the most vulnerable segment, with some consolidating into marketplace listings and others retreating into niche specialisation.

**VRL observation**: the Takealot/Amazon SA two-platform structure means no single platform owns the buyer relationship any more. This makes independent lead-gen layers structurally more valuable &mdash; because neither platform can dominate merchant acquisition in the way a single-platform monopoly would.

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **Amazon SA completed its first full operating year** in mid-2025 with widely reported continued category expansion and marketplace seller onboarding.
- **Takealot Blue Dot Sale** and **Black Friday 2024 + 2025** cycles continued to drive peak-season volume, with Takealot maintaining its position as the single most-watched SA retail calendar event.
- **Checkers Sixty60** expanded its delivery footprint and category range, reinforcing Shoprite&apos;s dominance in on-demand grocery.
- **BNPL adoption** continued through 2024-2025 across Payflex, Mobicred, Happy Pay and other providers, reshaping checkout conversion economics for mid-ticket categories.
- **Shopify Plus merchants** in SA grew in visible number through 2025, with several DTC brands crossing the R50M/year revenue mark.
- **POPIA enforcement** by the Information Regulator continued with published enforcement notices against non-compliant operators in specific sectors.
- **Logistics consolidation** continued with The Courier Guy and Aramex dominating national courier volume, while PUDO and Pargo expanded locker / pickup-point networks.

### Honest caveats for publication

- The "Amazon SA has reshaped the market" framing is supported by trade press commentary but the actual market share shift (Amazon share vs Takealot share post-launch) is not publicly disclosed by either company.
- CAC benchmarks in Section 4 are market-observed, not industry-surveyed.
- The R50M target in the Visio Commerce paper is a VRL projection based on lead volumes and prices, not an existing run-rate.
- Lead-gen in e-commerce is structurally different from other Visio Lead Gen verticals: it is high-volume, low-ticket, consumer-side intent-capture, not practitioner-side referral. The unit economics are closer to an affiliate network than a traditional B2B lead marketplace.
