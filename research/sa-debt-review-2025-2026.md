## 1. Market Structure — the NCR-regulated debt counsellor universe

South Africa's debt review market is one of the most tightly regulated consumer-protection regimes on the continent. The entire market is defined by the **National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA)** and administered by the **National Credit Regulator (NCR)**.

- The NCR maintains the **official register of debt counsellors** at ncr.org.za — every practising debt counsellor must be NCR-registered, hold a valid practice number, and comply with the Debt Counselling Association of South Africa (DCASA) guidelines.
- **Debt counsellors** (Section 44 of the NCA) are the only professionals permitted to perform **debt review** under Section 86 of the NCA &mdash; the statutory mechanism by which an over-indebted consumer can restructure their obligations to credit providers.
- **Debt administrators** are a separate category regulated under the Magistrates&apos; Court Act and should not be confused with debt counsellors; administrators charge higher fees and operate outside the NCA framework.
- The long tail of the market includes a handful of consolidators, fintech entrants (Meerkat, Debt Rescue), and legacy firms.

### Real NCR-registered debt counsellor brands

- **DebtBusters** — largest by volume, publishes the monthly DebtBusters Debt Index (used by Reuters and trade press as a headline indicator of SA consumer debt stress)
- **National Debt Counsellors (NDC)** — long-established national brand
- **NDRC (National Debt Review Center)** — digital-first player
- **Meerkat** — fintech-enabled player, Section 86 + insurance wraparound
- **Consumer Debt Help** — indie operator
- **DebtSafe** — long-standing counsellor

## 2. The Section 86 debt review business model

The economics of a debt review counsellor are defined by a fee table published by the NCR and by the fixed steps of the Section 86 process.

- **Section 86 application** — the consumer applies to a debt counsellor, who assesses over-indebtedness and issues a **Form 16** notification to credit providers and credit bureaus. At this point the consumer&apos;s credit record is flagged as &ldquo;under debt review&rdquo; and all listed accounts are frozen from further legal action.
- **Section 86(7) proposal** — the counsellor negotiates a restructured repayment schedule (extended term, reduced instalment, paused interest where possible) with each credit provider.
- **Section 86(8) court order / NCT application** — the restructured schedule is made a binding court order (via the Magistrates&apos; Court) or a National Consumer Tribunal order. Once ordered, the consumer makes a single consolidated monthly payment to a Payment Distribution Agent (PDA), which disburses to creditors per the order.
- **Counsellor fees** — charged at NCR-prescribed rates (application fee, restructuring fee, after-care monthly fee), typically coming out of the consumer&apos;s consolidated monthly payment. Per the NCR Fee Guidelines, the application fee is in the R50–R150 range plus an aftercare fee of a few hundred rand per month, with all fees capped.

**Implications for lead pricing**: a warm Section 86-ready lead that converts to a signed debt review is worth a multi-year after-care stream plus the upfront restructuring fee. Lead-market prices for debt counselling in SA are observed in the **R150–R500** band, with premium leads (high balance, multiple credit providers, high income relative to debt) fetching higher.

## 3. The Two-Pot Retirement System — the macro catalyst of 2024–2026

The single largest structural event in the SA consumer-credit market since 2020 is the **Two-Pot Retirement System**, which became effective **1 September 2024**. It has become inseparable from the debt review story, because it provides a lump-sum withdrawal mechanism that most users are directing at debt settlement.

- **Two-pot withdrawals hit R21 billion by early 2025** per SAnews, and climbed to **R57 billion by June 2025** per Moonstone Information Refinery citing Treasury and ASISA data.
- **Over 2.4 million valid withdrawal tax directives** were approved by SARS by end-January 2025.
- **79% of members who withdrew to settle debt** have stated an intention to withdraw again at the next available cycle &mdash; a repeat-withdrawal pattern that is now structural to SA household finance.
- **62% of withdrawal claims submitted from March 2026 onward are 3rd withdrawals**, up from 33% second and only 5% first-time &mdash; indicating that the population of two-pot withdrawers has stabilised into a core group of repeat users.
- **SARS recovered R1 billion in outstanding tax debt** via two-pot stop orders that were automatically deducted before the withdrawal was paid out &mdash; the largest single enforcement event in recent SARS collection history.

**VRL thesis**: two-pot is a **detectable debt signal** — the act of withdrawing retirement savings to settle debt is a high-confidence indicator that the household is already in stress and is at risk of entering formal debt review within 6–18 months. The window between withdrawal and Section 86 application is the single most valuable targeting window for debt counsellors.

## 4. Over-indebtedness — the macro backdrop

Two-pot did not cause SA&apos;s over-indebtedness problem; it made it visible. The underlying stress data is sobering.

- **40% of credit-active South Africans are in default** on one or more loans, per Eighty20&apos;s Q4 2025 Credit Stress Report.
- **R12 billion increase in overdue balances** in the final quarter of 2025 alone.
- Unsecured lending, vehicle finance, and retail store cards are the three credit categories with the fastest-rising arrears.
- The DebtBusters Debt Index is the most-cited barometer of consumer debt stress, reporting an ongoing uptrend in debt-to-income ratios at the application moment.

**VRL thesis**: the overlap between the two-pot withdrawer universe (2.4m+ consumers), the over-indebted-credit-active universe (~40% of credit-active SA), and the addressable debt review market (structurally an NCR-registered counsellor pool of several thousand practitioners) produces a **signal-detectable pool of 500k–1m consumers per year** who are realistic candidates for a Section 86 referral within a 12-month window.

## 5. Repeat withdrawal patterns and the 3rd-draw population

The most important shift in the two-pot population through 2025–2026 is the emergence of a **core repeat-withdrawer group**. This group is not a one-off stress population — it is an ongoing cohort for whom two-pot has become a recurring cash-management tool.

- **March 2026 withdrawal mix**: 5% first-time, 33% second-time, 62% third-time (Moonstone).
- **Repeat intent**: 79% of debt-settlement withdrawers say they intend to withdraw again.
- **Implication for debt review**: the repeat-withdrawer is almost definitionally no longer solvent under their current obligations — two-pot is being used as a bridge to insolvency rather than a one-off rescue. For a Section 86 referral, this cohort is the highest-intent pool in the entire SA credit-active population.

**VRL thesis**: a **signal engine that detects second and third two-pot withdrawals** (via financial-stress search patterns, consecutive low-balance cycles, and declared financial events) produces a pre-qualified debt review referral stream that is impossible to replicate through traditional advertising channels.

## 6. Provincial distribution and Gauteng concentration

Over-indebtedness in SA is concentrated in the provinces with the largest credit-active populations, and Gauteng dominates both.

- **Gauteng is the single largest over-indebted household pool** by absolute count, tracking its share of national GDP (~34%) and its higher-than-average credit-active population density.
- **East Rand, Vaal Triangle, and Soweto** are the highest-volume debt review catchment areas within Gauteng — mid-income earners on vehicle finance, retail store cards, and personal loans.
- **Sandton / Rosebank / Bryanston** represent a smaller but higher-fee debt review segment — higher average balances per case, higher counsellor fees, higher referral economics.

**VRL observation**: a Gauteng-only debt review lead engine addresses roughly **40% of the national addressable market** with a single provincial focus — a concentration that justifies the Visio Lead Gen Gauteng-first strategy.
