## 1. Market Structure — the CMS-regulated medical scheme universe

South Africa's medical aid industry is structurally bifurcated: open schemes accept any member, while restricted schemes serve defined employer or industry groups. Both are regulated by the **Council for Medical Schemes (CMS)** under the **Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998**.

- **The CMS** is the statutory regulator. It publishes the annual Council for Medical Schemes Industry Report, accredits brokers and administrators, and enforces the Prescribed Minimum Benefits (PMB) framework.
- **Open schemes** — the consumer-facing segment. The seven largest open schemes dominate the market by membership count: Discovery Health Medical Scheme, Momentum Health, Bonitas, Profmed, Fedhealth, Bestmed, and several smaller operators.
- **Restricted schemes** — closed to the general public, typically serving government employees (GEMS), mining (Sisonke, Platinum Health), banking (Bankmed), and other industry or employer groups.
- **Administrators** — the largest open-scheme administrator is **Medscheme** (Momentum Metropolitan), followed by Discovery Health (Pty) Ltd for the Discovery scheme.
- **Brokers** — medical scheme brokers must be both **CMS-accredited** and **FSCA Category I FSP-licensed** under FAIS. Every advice interaction between a broker and a consumer is regulated by both acts.

### Medical scheme membership — approximate scale

- The total membership of SA medical schemes sits in the region of **~9 million lives** (members + dependants) — informed estimate, to be verified against the current CMS Annual Report.
- Open schemes collectively serve approximately 4.5 million principal members; restricted schemes serve the balance, dominated by GEMS (~1.9 million principal members).
- Medical aid penetration in SA is **~16% of the population**, with the remainder reliant on the public health system.

## 3. The 2025–2026 contribution hike cycle — the switching window catalyst

The single most important market event of 2024–2026 in SA medical aid is the back-to-back Discovery contribution increase cycle, and the fact that it is now producing a permanent, high-salience switching window.

- **Discovery 2025**: weighted average contribution increase of **9.3%** effective 1 January 2025, per Moonstone Information Refinery. Core and Priority plans rose ~10%, Saver plans ~8.4%, Smart plans ~7.4%. Approximately 50% of members saw a rise of 8.4% or less; the other 50% saw above 8.4%.
- **Discovery 2026**: the scheme announced an unusual decision to **delay the 2026 contribution increase to 1 April 2026**, with a weighted average hike of **7.9%**. This is highly unusual &mdash; medical scheme contribution increases historically take effect on 1 January &mdash; and creates a second switching window mid-way through the benefit year.
- **Industry-wide 2025 cycle**: across the five largest open schemes, contribution increases ranged from **9.4% to 12.75%**, making Discovery's 9.3% actually moderate relative to its peers.
- The CMS publicly encouraged 2025 increases to be aligned with the Reserve Bank's inflation forecast of **4.4% plus reasonable estimates of benefit utilisation** &mdash; but actual increases came in substantially higher, reflecting claims inflation and PMB cost growth.

**VRL thesis**: the combination of **two consecutive years of mid-single-digit to double-digit increases** plus the unusual **April 2026 delayed Discovery hike** produces a **permanently open switching window** &mdash; the first time in a decade that members have seen two re-pricings within an 18-month period. This is the single biggest lead-gen opportunity in the SA medical aid broker market.

## 4. Gap cover — the adjacent high-growth market

Gap cover is a parallel product regulated separately under **short-term insurance demarcation regulations**, not under the Medical Schemes Act. It pays the shortfall between medical scheme rates and actual specialist or hospital tariffs — a material and growing exposure for most members.

- **Gap cover demarcation regulations** define the rules under which gap cover can be sold alongside medical aid. Gap cover is a short-term insurance product, regulated under the Short-Term Insurance Act and the FSCA.
- **Major gap cover providers** include Stratum Benefits, Sirago, Zestlife, Ambledown, Sanlam Gap, Liberty Gap, and Turnberry.
- **Typical benefit**: gap cover pays the difference between the scheme's tariff (typically 100% of scheme rate) and the specialist's charge (often 300–500% of scheme rate for specialists like anaesthetists, surgeons, and radiologists).
- **Market size**: gap cover is a **R20bn+ adjacent product market** — informed VRL estimate, to be verified against the most recent FSCA short-term insurance industry data.

## 5. Broker accreditation and the commission model

Medical aid brokers sit at the intersection of two regulatory regimes and earn commission from one while being accredited by the other.

- **CMS accreditation** — required for any broker advising on medical scheme selection. The CMS Broker Accreditation Division issues the accreditation number.
- **FSCA Category I FSP licence** — required under FAIS for any advice on financial products, including gap cover (which is a financial product under FAIS, whereas medical scheme membership is not, per the FAIS exclusion).
- **Commission** — set by regulation. Brokers earn a fixed monthly fee per principal member (the rate is published by the CMS and adjusted annually), capped as a percentage of gross contribution.
- **Gap cover commission** — set by the gap cover provider, typically in the 15–22% range on annual premium.
- **Employee Benefits Consultants (EBCs)** — a separate but overlapping category, serving corporate medical scheme clients. EBC remuneration is negotiated, not regulated.

**Real broker and EBC firms**: Stratum Benefits, Sirago, IFC Brokers, OPTIVEST, NMG Benefits, Alexforbes Health, Momentum Corporate.

## 6. The switching paradox — why most members never switch

Despite double-digit hikes, the switching rate between open medical schemes remains remarkably low. The CMS reports annual inter-scheme transfers in the low single-digit percent of total membership, despite the headline price pressure.

The reasons are structural:

1. **Waiting periods** — a new scheme can impose a general waiting period of up to 3 months and a condition-specific waiting period of up to 12 months for pre-existing conditions (per the Medical Schemes Act).
2. **Loss of accumulated benefit limits** — moving mid-benefit-year resets savings account balances and annual limits.
3. **Specialist network reshuffling** — many members stay because their preferred specialists contract with their current scheme.
4. **Complexity** — medical aid is famously opaque. Members cannot easily compare two schemes plan-to-plan, and most lack the technical literacy to do so.
5. **Fear of mis-selling** — members know the broker advising them earns a commission from the new scheme, and often stay put as a defensive action.

**VRL thesis**: the low switching rate is the broker opportunity, not the broker obstacle. A tool that **transparently compares schemes including waiting-period implications and specialist network overlap** removes most of the switching friction at the point of research, and converts the broker's job from cold outreach into a warm consultation with an already-informed prospect.

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **Discovery 2025 hike 9.3% weighted average** (1 January 2025) — the largest single price event of the year in open-scheme medical aid.
- **Discovery 2026 hike delayed to 1 April 2026, weighted average 7.9%** — an unusual mid-year re-pricing that splits the 2026 benefit year into two distinct pricing periods.
- **NHI Act legal challenges** continue through 2025–2026, with multiple industry associations (Board of Healthcare Funders, BHF; South African Private Practitioners Forum, SAPPF) challenging various provisions of the National Health Insurance Act. These challenges introduce regulatory uncertainty around the future of medical schemes themselves.
- **Medscheme / Momentum Metropolitan consolidation** continued through 2024–2025 as the administrator landscape tightens.
- **Gap cover product innovation** — several providers added oncology-specific riders and high-cost chronic condition add-ons.
- **Employer shift to low-cost benefit options (LCBOs)** — the CMS has been engaged in a long-running consultation on allowing schemes to offer lower-cost benefit options to lower-income members, separate from the PMB framework. This would expand the addressable medical aid market materially if it passes.
