## 1. Market Structure — attorneys, consultants, and corporate mobility firms

South Africa's immigration market is bifurcated by who is permitted to give visa advice and who is permitted to represent applicants formally before the Department of Home Affairs (DHA).

- **Immigration attorneys** — admitted attorneys who specialise in immigration law. Regulated by the **Legal Practice Council (LPC)** under the **Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014**. They may appear at the High Court, the Refugee Appeals Authority, and Home Affairs forums on their clients' behalf.
- **Immigration consultants** — non-attorney specialists. Most are accredited by professional associations (e.g. SAAMS, the South African Association of Migration Specialists) and may assist with applications but cannot litigate. They may not call themselves attorneys and operate under DHA registration where applicable.
- **Corporate mobility firms** — large, multi-jurisdictional firms serving corporate clients on global assignment, intra-company transfer, and tax-equalisation work. Examples include the in-house mobility teams of the Big Four accounting firms and standalone specialists.
- **Commercial law firms with immigration desks** — top-tier corporate firms that maintain immigration practices serving inbound corporate clients (Cliffe Dekker Hofmeyr, ENSafrica, Webber Wentzel, Bowmans).

## 2. The dual-flow market — outbound emigration and inbound expat intake

The SA immigration market is uniquely structured by two simultaneous, non-overlapping flows. Most practitioners serve only one of the two; a few large firms serve both.

### Outbound (SA citizens emigrating)

- Primary destinations: **United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand, Portugal, Canada, Ireland, the United Arab Emirates**.
- Driven by safety concerns, healthcare and education quality, currency stability, and (for skilled professionals) recognised-qualification pipelines.
- Practitioner work focuses on destination-country visa applications, NQF-equivalence documentation, financial emigration via SARS, and tax exit planning.
- **Volume**: famously divergent across sources. StatsSA reports do not capture emigration directly; UK ONS reports SA-born inbound; receiving countries report grant counts. Approximate informed range: **40,000–100,000 SA-born people emigrating per year** during peak outflow years (2018–2024). This range should be flagged as estimate-of-estimates and not relied upon.

### Inbound (foreign nationals entering SA)

- Primary segments: **SADC labour migration, intra-company transfers, critical skills professionals, retirees, study visas, spousal/relatives visas**.
- Practitioner work focuses on critical skills visa applications, intra-company transfer permits, work visa renewals, and PR (permanent residence) applications.
- The **Critical Skills Visa list** (last refreshed 2022, with 2025/2026 update under DHA consultation) defines the categories of in-demand skills that qualify for fast-track work visa processing.
- **Volume**: also imprecise. DHA published in 2024 that backlog clearance work was reducing turnaround times, but absolute application volumes are not consistently published.

## 3. The Critical Skills Visa list — the macro catalyst

The most concrete near-term catalyst in the SA inbound immigration market is the **Critical Skills Visa list refresh** under DHA consultation since 2024. The list defines which professional skills qualify for fast-track work visa processing under Section 19 of the Immigration Act.

- The list was last formally published in **February 2022** and contains approximately 100+ skill categories spanning ICT, engineering, healthcare, finance, agriculture, and academic fields.
- The 2025/2026 refresh is expected to **add categories driven by the renewable energy transition** (solar engineers, grid integration specialists), the digital economy (cloud architects, cybersecurity), and the healthcare workforce shortage (specialists, nursing).
- Each addition or removal triggers a 6–12 month wave of application activity from foreign nationals already in-country on other visa categories who are eligible to switch.

**VRL thesis**: the Critical Skills Visa list refresh is the **single most predictable inbound immigration catalyst** for the next 18 months. Any practitioner panel positioned to capture this wave at the moment of list publication has a structural lead-flow advantage over competitors.

## 4. Home Affairs digital visa rollout 2025 and the Trusted Employer Scheme

The **Department of Home Affairs digital visa modernisation programme** is the second major macro catalyst, driven by the Minister&apos;s commitment to clearing the backlog and modernising the application infrastructure.

- The DHA announced in 2024 a phased rollout of an **electronic visa adjudication system** (eVisa) for select corridors and visa categories, replacing manual paper-based processing for several types of inbound visa.
- A **Trusted Employer Scheme (TES)** was introduced in 2024 to allow multinational employers with a strong compliance record to fast-track work visa applications for their inbound staff. Participating employers must meet a set of compliance criteria and apply through the DHA.
- The **Points-Based System** for general work visas was proposed in DHA white papers during 2024-2025 and remains under consultation.
- **Backlog clearance** of long-pending visa applications was a stated priority through 2024-2025; DHA reported progress on long-standing backlogs but specific clearance figures are not consistently published.

**VRL observation**: the digital rollout combined with the TES and the points-based system represent the largest structural change to SA inbound immigration in over a decade. Practitioner workflow will shift materially &mdash; from paper-form completion toward eVisa portal navigation, document upload, and digital case management. Firms that adapt their service offering to the new system have a competitive advantage; firms that do not will lose volume.

## 5. Fee economics — the practitioner revenue model

The economics of immigration practice in SA are bimodal: high-volume consultant work at lower margins, and high-touch attorney work at much higher margins. **All figures below are informed market observations and should be flagged as estimates.**

| Service | Typical fee | Repeat | Practitioner type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial consultation | R1,500–R5,000 | One-off | All |
| Critical skills visa application | R15,000–R45,000 | One-off + renewals | Consultant or attorney |
| Spousal / relatives visa | R12,000–R30,000 | One-off | Consultant |
| Business visa | R25,000–R80,000 | One-off + renewal | Attorney |
| PR application | R20,000–R60,000 | One-off | Attorney or senior consultant |
| Appeal / judicial review | R30,000–R150,000+ | One-off | Attorney only |
| Corporate intra-company transfer | R20,000–R60,000 per case | Bulk per company | Corporate mobility firm |
| Financial emigration / tax exit | R15,000–R75,000 | One-off | Attorney + tax practitioner |

**Implications for lead pricing**: a warm lead that converts to a critical skills visa application generates **R15k–R45k** of practitioner revenue. At a 25% qualified-lead-to-engagement conversion rate, the break-even lead price is in the R3,750–R11,250 band. Lead-market prices for immigration services in SA are observed in the **R800–R3,000** band, with premium leads (PR applications, business visas, corporate engagements) fetching the top of the range.

## 6. High-net-worth emigration and financial emigration via SARS

A specific high-margin sub-segment of the outbound market is **high-net-worth emigration** &mdash; SA-resident taxpayers who are formally exiting the SARS tax net under the financial emigration framework.

- **Financial emigration** is administered by **SARS via tax residency cessation declarations**. The 2021 reforms removed the old SARB-based emigration concept and replaced it with a SARS tax residency framework.
- **Tax exit planning** for high-net-worth individuals involves capital gains tax on the deemed disposal of worldwide assets at the moment of cessation, planning around retirement annuity withdrawals, and management of trust structures.
- Practitioners serving this segment are typically **attorney + tax-practitioner partnerships**, with fees in the R75k+ range per case.
- **Volume**: small but consistent &mdash; widely cited at several thousand per year, but exact figures are not published.

**VRL thesis**: financial emigration is a small-volume high-fee segment that is highly defensible through reputation and referral. A signal layer that detects HNW emigration intent (large RA withdrawals, trust restructurings, asset realisation patterns) represents a premium-tier lead product within the immigration vertical &mdash; potentially R3,000-R5,000 per qualified lead.

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **Critical Skills Visa list refresh under DHA consultation** &mdash; the most-watched regulatory event in inbound immigration, with the new list expected to add renewable-energy and digital-economy categories.
- **eVisa rollout** &mdash; phased introduction of digital visa adjudication for select corridors and categories.
- **Trusted Employer Scheme (TES)** &mdash; introduced 2024, allowing compliant multinational employers to fast-track work visas for inbound staff.
- **Backlog clearance progress** &mdash; DHA reported progress on long-pending visa applications through 2024-2025; specific clearance numbers not consistently published.
- **UK and Australia continue as the largest outbound destinations** for SA emigrants, with ongoing visa programme adjustments at the destination-country level.
- **Financial emigration framework stable** &mdash; the SARS tax residency cessation regime continues to operate as established by the 2021 reforms.
- **Corporate mobility activity** &mdash; intra-company transfer activity remains structurally tied to the SA renewable energy and mining capex cycles, both of which were elevated through 2025.

## 8. What We Could Verify vs. What We Could Not

### Verified (high confidence)
- LPC is the regulator for immigration attorneys under the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014
- DHA administers visa processing, with consultants/attorneys operating in advisory and representation capacities
- Real practitioners: Xpatweb, Intergate Immigration, IBN Immigration Solutions, Eisenberg & Associates (est 1997), Craig Smith & Associates, Cliffe Dekker Hofmeyr, Sable International
- The Critical Skills Visa list was last formally published in February 2022 and is under refresh consultation
- The Trusted Employer Scheme was introduced in 2024
- Financial emigration is administered by SARS under the tax residency cessation framework (post-2021 reforms)
- The eVisa rollout is a phased programme by DHA

### Not independently verified in this pass — pull before publication
- Exact outbound emigration volumes per year (the 40,000–100,000 range is an estimate-of-estimates)
- Exact inbound visa application volumes per year by category
- Exact number of categories on the 2022 Critical Skills Visa list (the "100+" figure is an approximation)
- Exact eVisa rollout coverage (which corridors and which visa categories are live)
- Exact TES participation count
- Practitioner fee bands in Section 5 are observational, not industry-surveyed
- Financial emigration volumes per year
- DHA backlog clearance numbers

### Honest caveats for publication
- Outbound emigration volume is **the most contested data point in the SA immigration market**. Any paper or marketing that quotes a specific number must cite the specific source (StatsSA, UK ONS, Homecoming Revolution, NWU studies) and acknowledge the divergence across sources.
- The Critical Skills Visa list refresh timing is uncertain and should be framed as "expected" rather than "scheduled".
- The "renewable energy + digital economy" projection for the new Critical Skills list is a VRL thesis based on the structural skills shortages in those sectors, not a DHA announcement.
- Practitioner fee bands in Section 5 are illustrative and should not be quoted as industry benchmarks.
- The R50M ARR target in the Visio Visa paper is a VRL projection.
