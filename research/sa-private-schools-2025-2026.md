## 1. Market Structure — the JSE-listed duopoly and the long tail

South Africa's private school market has a clean two-tier structure: two JSE-listed operators who dominate the mid-fee mass market, and a long tail of elite indie schools, franchised networks, and community foundations that serve narrower segments.

- **ADvTECH (JSE: ADH)** is the single largest private education group in SA. It operates Crawford International, Trinity House, Abbotts College, Maragon, Junior College, and the mid-fee Pinnacle College network, alongside tertiary and resourcing businesses. Press coverage in 2025 described ADvTECH as a "R21 billion private school empire" — a figure that reflects enterprise-value commentary rather than an independently verified ARR, and should be flagged accordingly.
- **Curro Holdings (JSE: COH)** is the other listed operator. Curro reported **95.49% NSC pass rate** and **99.5% IEB pass rate for 1,525 learners across 30 campuses** in its 2024 results (per news24 / trade press), positioning itself as the affordable-to-mid-fee network with national reach.
- **SPARK Schools** operates **18 schools total (16 primary + 2 high schools)**, concentrated in Gauteng with a small Western Cape footprint. Tech-enabled delivery model at an affordable fee point.
- **Reddam House** is a premium-tier indie network owned by Inspired Education Group, with schools in Gauteng, Cape Town and Durban.
- **Independent elite schools** &mdash; Bishops Diocesan College, St Andrews, St Stithians, Roedean, St Mary&apos;s (Waverley), Kingsmead, Michaelhouse, Hilton College, St John&apos;s College &mdash; serve the top fee band and compete almost entirely on academic reputation, sporting tradition, and alumni network.
- **Affordable Christian, Jewish, Muslim and Montessori networks** form a large, culturally specific sub-market with fees materially below the Curro-Pinnacle band.

## 2. Segmentation by fee band — four distinct markets pretending to be one

"Private schools" in SA is not one market; it is four distinct markets with different buyers, different decision drivers, and different lead-gen economics.

| Tier | Fee band (annual) | Examples | Buyer profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Elite** | R120k–R300k | Bishops, St Andrews, Roedean, Michaelhouse, Hilton College, St John&apos;s, Kingsmead | HNW families, multi-generational legacy, boarding demand |
| **Premium** | R80k–R150k | Crawford International, Trinity House, Reddam House | Upper-middle income, IEB pathway, international curriculum interest |
| **Mid-fee** | R40k–R90k | Curro (mid-fee), Maragon, Abbotts College | Middle income, public-school alternative, exam-results-driven |
| **Affordable** | R15k–R45k | Curro (low-fee), Pinnacle College, SPARK Schools, Christian/Jewish/Muslim/Montessori indies | Aspirational middle class, religious/cultural alignment, public-school safety concerns |

**Implications for lead pricing**: fee-band is the single most important filter in the vertical. An affordable-tier lead (R300 per qualified lead) served to an elite school is useless; an elite-tier lead (R1,000+ per qualified lead) served to a R25k-fee affordable school destroys the school&apos;s unit economics on the enrolment. A credible signal layer must segment at the fee-band level before any other routing decision.

## 5. Intake cycles — January primary + July secondary + mid-year transfers

Enrolment in SA private schools is structured around two calendar windows plus a long tail of mid-year transfers.

- **January primary intake** &mdash; the dominant enrolment window. Parents typically begin researching in the previous September-October, attend open days November-December, submit applications by the beginning of the fourth term, and receive offers in late November or early December for a January start. Approximately **60%+ of new enrolments fall into this window** (VRL informed estimate, verify against school-level admissions data before publication).
- **July secondary intake** &mdash; a smaller second window for mid-year transfers, typically serving relocating families, parents withdrawing from underperforming schools mid-year, and students returning from abroad.
- **Mid-year transfers** &mdash; a rolling trickle of individual transfers throughout the academic year, typically driven by family relocation, bullying concerns, academic underperformance, or financial pressure forcing a downgrade to a lower fee band.

**VRL thesis**: the January window is entirely predictable and calendar-driven. A signal engine that captures school-research intent from April-August of the preceding year has a 4-8 month lead-time on the decision, and can deliver pre-qualified leads to schools months before the parent has made contact with any school directly.

## 6. The public-to-private drift — the demand engine

Every published article on SA private school growth points to the same structural demand engine: parents exiting government schools in search of better quality, safer environments, and more reliable exam outcomes.

- **Public school quality concerns** &mdash; underperforming schools, infrastructure failures, teacher shortages, and safety concerns in many urban and peri-urban government schools.
- **Matric results disparity** &mdash; IEB pass rates consistently exceed 98%, while the DBE NSC pass rate sits in the low-to-mid 80s. For a fee-paying parent, the exam-outcome gap is a powerful justification for the annual fee.
- **Boarding demand** &mdash; rising boarding enrolment at elite schools reflects increasing parental willingness to send children away from under-performing local schools.
- **Relocation patterns** &mdash; internal migration from smaller towns to Gauteng metros and semigration from Gauteng to Cape Town / KZN North Coast drives continuous private-school lead flow as families arrive in new cities and need to enrol children immediately.
- **Curro and ADvTECH own the "affordable alternative" conversation** &mdash; together they represent the largest organised capacity-expansion story in SA education, with new campus openings year on year.

- Exact proportion of new enrolments in January vs July intake (the 60%+ January figure is a VRL estimate)

### Honest caveats for publication
- The "R21 billion private school empire" figure for ADvTECH is **press-reported, not financially defined**. It should not be quoted as a revenue, ARR, or market cap figure without verification against the underlying source article and ADvTECH&apos;s own financial disclosures.
- Curro and ADvTECH NSC and IEB pass rates are high by construction &mdash; these schools admit students who are already likely to succeed, and the pass rates are partly a selection effect, not purely a teaching-quality effect. Any paper content should acknowledge this.
- Fee bands in Section 2 are illustrative and span annual fees that include tuition only; they exclude boarding, uniforms, transport, trips, and other extras, which can add 20-50% to the true annual cost of enrolment.
- The 60%+ January intake figure is a VRL estimate; actual distribution varies materially between primary and high-school enrolments.
- The R40M ARR target in the Visio Schools paper is a VRL projection.
- Private school decisions involve children&apos;s data, which is treated as Special Personal Information under POPIA; the ethical bar for any lead-gen activity in this vertical is materially higher than in adult consumer verticals.
