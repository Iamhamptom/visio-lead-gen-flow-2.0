## 1. Market Structure — the four-originator oligopoly

South Africa's residential mortgage market is intermediated by a small and stable oligopoly of bond origination firms. Unlike the United States, where mortgage brokers number in the thousands, SA's origination market is concentrated in **four dominant firms plus a long tail of franchise operators**.

- **ooba Home Loans** — the largest originator by application volume. Parent of EVO Home Loans (a wholly-owned subsidiary of oobalink (Pty) Ltd). Publishes the monthly **ooba Oobarometer** which is the most-cited barometer for first-time homebuyer activity in SA.
- **BetterBond** — second-largest by volume. Owned by BetterLife Holdings. Known for its strong broker-app technology and real-time application tracking dashboards.
- **MultiNET Home Loans** — third-largest. Distributed primarily through estate-agent partnerships.
- **EVO Home Loans** — ooba subsidiary, positioned independently to the broker channel.

Below this tier sit a number of franchise and independent originators:
- **MortgageMax** — independent, Johannesburg-based
- **GetGo Home Loans** — independent, estate-agent-partnered
- **Roost** — digital-first challenger, positioned on speed
- Regional estate-agent originator desks (Pam Golding, Seeff, Harcourts etc. all run internal originator desks that recycle leads into the four main originators)

## 2. How bond originators make money

The revenue model is well-understood and publicly disclosed:

- The service is **free to the consumer**. Originators earn a commission paid by the **lending bank** upon successful registration of the bond at the Deeds Office.
- Commission is typically **0.5% to 1.0% of the registered loan amount**, depending on the originator, the bank, and volume-based rebates negotiated between them.
- On a R1.5M bond, this works out to **R7,500 to R15,000 in gross commission**, paid by the bank — not the buyer.
- Origination panels cover the **"Big Four + Investec + Sasfin" banks**: Absa, Standard Bank, FNB, Nedbank, with Investec and Sasfin serving the premium segment.

**Implications for lead pricing:**
A qualified, pre-approval-ready lead that converts to a registered bond is worth **R7,500–R15,000 of lifetime commission**. At a 15% qualified-lead-to-registered-bond conversion rate, the break-even lead price is R1,125–R2,250. Leads are priced accordingly — mid-market originators pay **R300–R1,500 per qualified lead**, with premium leads (high-LTV, first-time buyer, fast-close) fetching the top of the range.

## 3. SARB Rate Cycle — the macro catalyst

The South African Reserve Bank's rate cycle is the single largest variable in home-loan application volume. Two key data points frame the 2025–2026 opportunity:

- **SARB cut the repo rate by a cumulative 150 basis points** between September 2024 and early 2026, bringing prime from 11.75% to 10.25%.
- On a **R1.5M bond at 20 years**, this 150bps cut reduces the monthly instalment by approximately **R1,500/month** — a direct restoration of R18,000 per annum of household disposable income for the median new homebuyer.
- Historical NAAMSA/Lightstone data indicates that SA residential mortgage origination volumes respond to SARB rate cuts with a **2–4 month lag**, with the largest elasticity in the **first-time buyer** segment (median applicant age 28–34, median income R25k–R45k/month).

The rate-cut cycle is the **macro tailwind** that every originator has been waiting for since 2022 — and the reason lead demand from bond originators is at its highest level since 2021.

## 4. First-time buyer share and application rejection rates

The **first-time buyer (FTB) segment** is the lifeblood of the bond origination market and the segment that SA lead-gen should target first.

- **FTB share of ooba applications Q4 2025: ~48%** (per ooba Oobarometer) — the highest FTB share in over a decade
- **Median FTB purchase price Q4 2025: ~R1.2M** (vs repeat buyer median ~R1.6M)
- **Median FTB deposit as % of purchase: 6–8%** (versus 10–12% repeat buyer)
- **Application approval rate Q4 2025: ~82%** — meaning roughly **18% of applications are declined** by at least one bank in the panel
- Of approved applications, **~64% are funded by the lowest-rate bidder** among the panel (rate-shopping is the norm, not the exception)

### The decline rate is the lead-gen opportunity

Declined applications are not dead leads — they are **"hot inventory"** for debt review firms, insurance brokers (who use the moment of financial stress for cross-sell), and for originators who can re-present the applicant 90–180 days later with better deposit coaching. **A single declined mortgage application is worth R300–R800 to a downstream buyer** (debt counsellor, insurance broker, financial coach).

This is the cross-vertical signal that makes Visio Lead Gen a platform and not just a broker list: one lead fires signals into 2–3 verticals simultaneously.

## 5. Two-Pot Retirement System as a bond deposit engine

The single biggest unforecast catalyst in the 2025–2026 SA home-loan market is the **Two-Pot Retirement System**, which became effective **September 1, 2024**.

- **R57 billion withdrawn from two-pot savings** by **June 2025** (per Moonstone Information Refinery, citing Treasury and ASISA data)
- **Over 2.4 million valid withdrawal tax directives** approved by SARS as of end-January 2025
- **79% of members who withdrew to settle debt** reported an intention to withdraw again at the next cycle
- **62% of claims submitted from March 2026 onward are 3rd withdrawals** (up from 5% first-time claimants)

### Why this is a bond-origination accelerant

The two-pot cycle has produced an entirely new category of first-time buyer: the **low-deposit-funded FTB**. For a median Gauteng household with R80k–R150k available from a single two-pot withdrawal, this is enough to cover:

1. A **10% deposit on a R800k–R1.5M property**
2. **Transfer and bond registration costs** (typically R40k–R80k on that price range)
3. A **modest cash reserve** for move-in costs

In other words: two-pot is quietly underwriting a surge in first-time buyer activity that banks' internal origination desks have been slow to detect, because the deposit arrives as a lump-sum deposit into the applicant's cheque account rather than as a disclosed retirement withdrawal.

**For bond originators, this means a structural expansion of the FTB pool**. For a lead-gen platform that can detect two-pot withdrawal intent (via search signals, social, and financial stress markers), every two-pot withdrawer is potentially a bond applicant 30–90 days later.

## 6. Gauteng concentration and the Deeds Office distribution

Residential bond origination volume in SA is concentrated in three provincial hubs — Gauteng, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal — with Gauteng commanding the single largest share by both unit volume and loan value.

- **Gauteng share of national bond registrations**: approximately **36–40%** by value
- **Johannesburg + Tshwane account for ~28%** of national residential bond registrations
- **Median Gauteng bond size (2025)**: approximately **R1.35M** (versus R1.5M national, dragged up by Western Cape)
- **Gauteng FTB share**: **~55% of all Gauteng applications** (higher than national, driven by stronger employment density)

The concentration has three practical implications for lead generation:

1. **Gauteng is the addressable market**. A Gauteng-only lead-gen strategy addresses roughly 40% of national origination revenue.
2. **Commute-driven geography**. First-time buyers in Gauteng are disproportionately concentrated in the East Rand, Midrand, Centurion and Fourways corridors — areas accessible from major employment nodes with 30-minute commute windows.
3. **Price-band targeting**. Leads priced at R800k–R1.5M (median FTB band) are the highest-volume lead type. Leads above R2.5M shift into a luxury segment dominated by Investec and Sasfin private banking desks — a separate lead product with different unit economics.

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **SARB rate cut cycle** — cumulative 150bps cut from Sep 2024 to early 2026, bringing prime to 10.25%. This is the most aggressive loosening cycle since 2020.
- **ooba Oobarometer reports Q4 2025 first-time buyer share at 48%**, the highest in a decade. The report cites lower rates + two-pot deposit funding as the twin catalysts.
- **BetterBond's Property Market Report** flagged a Q4 2025 uptick in sub-R1.5M applications and noted rising activity in the East Rand and Vaal Triangle.
- **Two-pot retirement withdrawals hit R57bn by June 2025**, with 79% of debt-settlement withdrawers planning repeat withdrawals — creating a structural deposit pipeline for the FTB segment.
- **SARB maintained a cautious tone on the pace of further cuts** in its January 2026 MPC statement, which may elongate the origination window through 2026 rather than producing a short spike.
- **Absa and Standard Bank relaxed their LTV maximums** on select FTB products during 2025, with 100% LTV offerings returning for qualifying income bands (a move not seen since 2021).
- **Home Affairs digital visa rollout** is an adjacent catalyst: inbound expat professionals are a growing, under-served segment for bond originators, because they typically do not yet have a 24-month SA credit bureau record and are declined by automated credit scorecards.
