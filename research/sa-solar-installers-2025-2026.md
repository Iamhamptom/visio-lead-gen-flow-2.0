## 1. Market Structure — the SAPVIA accreditation tier and the long tail of installers

South Africa's solar installation market is a long-tail industry with thousands of operators across a quality spectrum. The structure has crystallised around a **voluntary accreditation pyramid**:

- **SAPVIA (South African Photovoltaic Industry Association)** — the dominant industry body. SAPVIA-accredited installers represent the quality tier of the market; accreditation requires documented installation track record, technical qualifications, and adherence to SAPVIA's code of practice.
- **PV GreenCard** — the quality-certification programme backed by SAPVIA, SAPVIA member companies, and industry training partners. A PV GreenCard issued against a specific installation is the closest thing the SA market has to a warranty-of-quality on a PV system.
- **Non-accredited installers** — a long tail of smaller operators, electricians, and single-van operators serving the residential market, particularly in the sub-5kWp category.

## 2. Installation economics — CapEx vs PPA vs lease

The unit economics of a solar project depend more on the financing structure than on the hardware choice, and this has become the central strategic question for commercial installers.

- **CapEx (outright purchase)** — the traditional model. Customer pays upfront, owns the system outright, captures the full electricity savings, and claims any applicable tax benefits. Target market: households with cash, SME owner-occupiers, well-capitalised corporates.
- **PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)** — third-party-owned systems sold to the host as a per-kWh electricity contract at a discount to Eskom's commercial tariff. Target market: C&I hosts without capital, REITs, industrial estates. Typical PPA term: 10–20 years.
- **Lease / subscription** — a variant of PPA structured as a monthly fixed fee for a sized system. Target market: residential, SMEs that prefer OpEx to CapEx.
- **Section 12B Accelerated Depreciation** — under the Income Tax Act, renewable energy assets qualify for accelerated depreciation (100% in year 1 for qualifying assets). This is the single largest tax lever for commercial solar payback and was expanded temporarily for the period 1 March 2023 to 28 February 2025. Post-2025 reversion back to the standard 50/30/20 three-year regime is a drag on C&I economics.

### Retail panel pricing

Residential solar panels retail in the range of **R1,200–R3,500 per panel**, depending on wattage, brand, and bulk discount availability. Retail markup adds roughly 15–30%, and installation companies often stack a further 40–60% markup on panel hardware for a fully fitted system.

## 3. Eskom tariff hikes as the macro catalyst

The entire residential and commercial solar industry exists because grid electricity in South Africa has become unaffordable on a pathway that every customer can now see in black and white on their monthly statement.

- **Eskom's 2025 tariff increase** was reported in trade press at approximately **12.7%** effective 1 April 2025 — flag this figure for verification against the final NERSA MYPD5 determination before publication.
- **Eskom's MYPD5 (Multi-Year Price Determination 5)** covers the period FY2026 onwards. NERSA approves each annual adjustment; the approval cycle is the single most-watched calendar event in the SA solar industry.
- **Cumulative tariff pressure** since 2008 has been extreme — residential cents-per-kWh has approximately tripled in nominal terms while hovering at ~2x in real terms.
- **Load-shedding legacy** — even with load-shedding having paused through late 2024 into 2025, the installed base of diesel generators and residential backup batteries remains huge, and the replacement cycle of those interim backup solutions back into full solar-plus-storage is an ongoing pipeline.

**VRL thesis**: the combination of visible annual tariff hikes + a sunset on Section 12B accelerated depreciation + continued grid unreliability produces a **permanent, calendar-driven pipeline** of commercial solar intent that is detectable three to six months before a contract close.

## 4. C&I versus residential — the strongest-performing segment

Per the SAPVIA September 2025 distributed generation trends report, **Commercial & Industrial (C&I) SSEG** is currently the strongest-performing segment of the South African solar market.

| Segment | Typical system size | Ticket size | Payback | Finance model |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Residential | 3–10 kWp | R80k–R300k | 4–7 years | CapEx + rent-to-own |
| C&I SSEG (≤1 MW) | 50 kWp–1 MW | R500k–R20m | 3–6 years | CapEx + PPA + lease |
| C&I Large-scale (>1 MW) | 1–10 MW+ | R20m–R200m+ | 5–8 years | PPA + project finance |
| Utility-scale | 10 MW+ | R200m+ | 7–12 years | PPA + project finance |

**Why C&I is the strongest performer**: the combination of visible tariff hikes, Section 12B tax benefits (while they lasted in their expanded form), credible PPA counterparties, and 3–6 year payback windows produces the cleanest IRR story in the SA market. Residential is fragmented, utility-scale is capital-gated; C&I is the fat middle.

## 5. Load-shedding legacy and the backup-power replacement pipeline

The years of Stage 2–6 load-shedding between 2019 and 2024 produced a huge installed base of diesel generators, portable inverters, and small residential battery systems. Most of those were interim measures intended to bridge a temporary crisis. The structural demand for a permanent replacement — solar + storage, grid-tied where possible — remains.

- **Diesel generator replacement pipeline**: every household and SME that bought a diesel generator in 2022–2023 is carrying a running fuel cost that a solar-plus-battery replacement eliminates. This is a definable, detectable intent pool.
- **UPS / portable battery replacement**: mid-tier residential buyers who bought 1–3 kWh portable battery solutions during load-shedding are now candidates for a full roof-mounted system.
- **Eskom grid instability** — even with load-shedding paused, grid frequency excursions, unplanned outages, and rolling local faults continue. The "grid unreliability" narrative has not disappeared; it has just become less televised.

**VRL thesis**: solar intent is a **stacked signal market** — customers accumulate partial solutions (generator → UPS → portable battery → small PV → full system) and each step creates a detectable intent signal for the next.

## 6. Gauteng concentration + East Rand industrial demand

Gauteng is the single largest provincial solar market by both residential volume and commercial pipeline.

- **Residential concentration**: higher-income suburbs in Johannesburg North (Sandton, Bryanston, Fourways, Midrand), Pretoria East, and Centurion dominate sub-20 kWp residential installs.
- **C&I concentration**: **East Rand and Ekurhuleni** — Germiston, Isando, Kempton Park, Boksburg, Alberton — are the industrial solar pipeline hotspots, driven by warehouse roofs, light-industrial estates, and the OR Tambo logistics corridor.
- **Corporate rooftops**: Gauteng office parks and A-grade commercial buildings (Sandton, Rosebank, Waterfall) are the target for REIT-financed PPA installations.

A Gauteng-focused solar lead engine addresses approximately **35–40% of national solar installation revenue** (VRL estimate — flag for verification).

## 7. Recent significant moves (2025 → early 2026)

- **Eskom tariff hike ~12.7%** (widely reported, verify against final NERSA determination) effective 1 April 2025 — the most significant single-year tariff move in recent memory.
- **Section 12B expanded tax regime expired 28 February 2025**, reverting to the older 50/30/20 regime for renewable assets. This has tightened C&I payback calculations from early 2025 onwards and is a known tailwind for pre-deadline installation intent.
- **SAPVIA September 2025 trends report** flagged C&I SSEG as the strongest-performing segment.
- **Residential rent-to-own / subscription offerings** from Hohm Energy, Versofy, and others have moved residential pricing competition from hardware toward financing structure.
- **REIT rooftop PPAs** have expanded materially across Growthpoint, Redefine, and Vukile portfolios through 2024–2025, turning large industrial landlords into solar counterparties.
- **Battery storage attachment rates** have risen sharply as lithium-iron-phosphate pricing has fallen — most new Gauteng residential installs now include some storage.
- **Eskom Feed-In Tariff** policy updates — the terms on which SSEG installations can sell surplus back to the grid remain in development at NERSA/Eskom level; this is the biggest regulatory uncertainty for the 50–500 kWp segment.
